Research Assistant

NCSA, UIUC - detecting safety incidents in video for assisted-living monitoring, where a false alarm has a human cost.

What I did

  • Worked with Prof. Volodymyr Kindratenko on an AI system for detecting safety incidents in video - altercations and theft or robbery indicators - for care-facility monitoring.
  • Benchmarked and tuned pre-trained detection and classification models, tracking precision and recall and reducing false positives through thresholding and scenario-specific filtering.
  • Designed an alerting and logging layer to surface high-confidence incidents in assisted-living environments and support downstream review and response.

Context

This was my first research role, at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications, and the setting made it a different problem than the benchmark suggests. Care facilities are homes. The system is watching people in the place they live, and the consequence of an alert is a staff member interrupting a resident’s day.

So the work was not really about pushing a detection score. It was about deciding what counted as an incident at all, and about building the layer between a model output and a human being asked to go look.

What I took away

Precision and recall are not symmetric in deployment, and the benchmark will not tell you which way. A missed altercation and a false alarm are both errors on a confusion matrix and nothing alike in a care facility. Thresholding and scenario-specific filtering were the tools, but the actual work was arriving at a defensible position on which error the system should prefer to make - a question the data has no opinion about.

Most of the system was not the model. The detector was pre-trained. What had to be designed was everything around it: what gets logged, what rises to an alert, what evidence a reviewer sees, and what happens to an incident nobody confirms. I arrived expecting the research to be in the network and found it in the plumbing.
