Teaching · ECE 120 & ECE 110, UIUC · 2023 – Present

Teaching Assistant

ECE 120 and ECE 110, UIUC - computer architecture discussion and electronics labs, for cohorts of 400+ and 300+.

What I did

ECE 120 - Introduction to Computing. Undergraduate assistant since August 2023.

  • Instructed and supported over 400 undergraduates in foundational computer architecture - logic design, state machines, assembly language and memory systems - across post-lecture discussion and lab sessions.
  • Guided student teams through digital circuit simulations, simple processor implementations, and assembly-level programming, connecting classroom theory to something that runs.
  • Collaborated with course instructors to develop and refine assignments, quizzes and lab exercises aimed at conceptual understanding rather than recall.
  • Held office hours and answered online forums, supporting students individually where the lecture format could not reach them.

ECE 110 - Introduction to Electronics. Lab course assistant, August 2023 to May 2024.

  • Instructed over 300 students in hands-on labs covering electrical circuits, analog and digital components, and the operation of integrated circuits.
  • Demonstrated and supervised bench equipment - oscilloscopes, function generators, multimeters, power supplies - so measurements were trustworthy before conclusions were drawn from them.
  • Troubleshot student designs in real time and graded lab reports with feedback aimed at documentation and design methodology, not just the right answer.

Context

ECE 120 and ECE 110 are the first courses in the sequence, which means the room is not full of people who already think like engineers - it is full of people deciding whether they want to. Both courses are large, and the assistant is the layer where a lecture that made sense in the abstract meets a circuit that does not work.

Teaching them at the same time was useful in a way I did not plan: ECE 120 is abstraction going down toward the hardware, ECE 110 is hardware coming up toward abstraction, and the students meeting in the middle are the same students.

What I took away

You do not understand something until you have watched thirty people misunderstand it in thirty different ways. Explaining state machines once is a summary of what you know. Explaining them to a room is a map of where your own model is thin, because the questions land exactly on the parts you had been gliding over.

The explanation that works is the one that survives being asked "why" three times. A good analogy gets a nod and stops there. Understanding shows up as the next question, so I stopped optimising for the moment students look satisfied.

It is also the most direct reason I write things up the way this site does - if the reasoning is not legible to someone who was not there, it has not really been communicated.
