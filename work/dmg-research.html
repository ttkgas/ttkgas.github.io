Research Intern

Data Mining Group, UIUC - measuring how far a closed-schema LLM gets on chemistry extraction before it genuinely needs labels.

What I did

  • Research intern in Prof. Jiawei Han’s Data Mining Group, working on structured information extraction from chemistry patents: turning reaction prose into a machine-readable, ontology-grounded closed schema.
  • Built the extraction and evaluation harness end to end and scored every experiment with the official ChEMU 2020 scorers, so no result on the project rests on a metric I defined myself.
  • Established with numbers where prompt engineering stops paying: four rounds of schema-definition refinement saturated within ±0.02 exact F1, while seven-shot examples moved NER F1 by +0.158 in a single step.
  • Reached 0.910 exact F1 on held-out documents for entity recognition against a supervised leaderboard ceiling of 0.957, and separated the two tasks by where their remaining error actually lives - NER fails on span boundaries, event extraction fails on chemistry judgement.

Context

Chemistry patents describe reactions in prose, and the field’s best results come from supervised models fine-tuned on hand-labelled corpora. Labels are the expensive part, and the question worth asking is therefore not "can an LLM extract" - it plainly can - but how much of the remaining gap is closable without labels at all.

That framing is what made the work a research project rather than an engineering one, and it is what set the discipline: every change had to be scored the same way, on the same held-out set, with the same official tooling, or it could not be compared to the change before it.

What I took away

Negative results are results, and they are cheaper than the alternative. A two-pass detect-then-link architecture for event extraction bought +0.017 F1 for double the cost and double the latency, so it was abandoned. Establishing that with a number took a day; not establishing it would have meant carrying the complexity indefinitely on the assumption it was helping.

The measurement infrastructure was the actual enabler. Wiring the official scorers in early felt like overhead at the time. It is the reason there is a saturation curve to point at instead of an anecdote, and the reason the held-out drop between the two tasks - 0.008 for NER, 0.058 for event extraction - could be read as a signal about which one deserves fine-tuning, rather than dismissed as noise.

Examples beat rules, and the crossover is measurable. Four rounds of writing better definitions were worth less than one round of showing the model seven worked examples. Knowing exactly where that crossover sits is worth more than either technique.
