What I did

  • Architected an end-to-end MLOps pipeline - dataset creation, augmentation, training, validation - for a real-time object-detection system in PyTorch and YOLO.
  • Led the project from start to finish (TRL 1 to 7) in four months, deploying a quantized model on an NVIDIA Jetson that verified wheelchair securement with 95% accuracy and a near 10× inference speedup (3 → 30 FPS).
  • Revived a stalled R&D initiative by developing a novel 6-DoF robotic arm - complete mechanical assembly in CAD (Fusion 360) plus a kinematic model in ROS using D-H parametrization - advancing it from TRL 1 to 4.
  • Co-inventor on 2 U.S. patents filed for a novel computer-vision and robotic-arm securement system.
  • Ran a comparative study across inductance, ultrasonic, and ToF sensing for redundant verification; the inductance-based recommendation was accepted by the team and taken to the Board of Directors.

Context

BraunAbility builds wheelchair-accessible vehicles, and securement - whether a wheelchair is actually locked down before the vehicle moves - is a safety function, not a convenience feature. That framing changes the engineering. A verification system in this domain does not get to be usually right.

Two of the four workstreams started from nothing: the detection system went from an idea to something running on a vehicle, and the robotic arm was a stalled R&D initiative that had not moved. The sensor study was different again - the deliverable was not a system but a recommendation that other people would act on, which meant the argument had to survive scrutiny from a team that had already been living with the problem longer than I had.

What I took away

Latency was the requirement; accuracy was the headline. 95% is the number people quote, but the one that decided whether the system shipped was 30 FPS. A verification that arrives after the vehicle is already moving is not a verification at any accuracy. Quantizing to reach that rate cost a little accuracy and bought the entire deployment, which is not a trade you can evaluate from a metric table.

TRL is a ladder of evidence, not a schedule. Moving 1 to 7 in four months was not a matter of working faster - each rung is a specific claim someone else has to be able to check, and most of the effort goes into producing that evidence rather than into the thing itself.

A recommendation is a deliverable. The sensor study produced no code. Comparing inductance, ultrasonic and ToF honestly, and then defending one choice well enough that it went to the Board, taught me more about engineering communication than any of the systems work did.
